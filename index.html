Read more about me...

In the midst of the Cambridge Analytica scandal and ProPublica’s COMPAS exposé, I first started coding. It wasn’t the potential of launching the next big startup that drove me, but the scandals themselves: the realization that technology was shaping society in ways that often went unquestioned. Reading The Alignment Problem cemented my decision to study computer science. It seemed like only by deeply understanding how algorithms and systems work, I can properly criticize and hold them accountable.

Studying computer science has been both challenging and rewarding. I’ve always loved math, science and the puzzle-solving side of coding, but I found myself drawn less to building products and more to questioning them. One moment that stuck with me was a conversation with a professor researching autonomous vehicle algorithms. When I asked how ethical trade-offs factored into his work, he replied that such judgement belonged to philosophers or lawyers, not engineers. That distinction revealed the limits of my field and sharpened my desire to bridge logic with ethics.

That curiosity led me to journalism. My first years at SNU were devoted to The SNU Quill, the university’s one and only independent English publication. My first article examined pro-eating disorder communities online and the flaws of social media moderation. I was quickly promoted to editor and later editor-in-chief of the 82nd edition, where I oversaw the entire editorial process. coverage of timely issues, such as the student assembly following President Yoon’s martial law declaration, which further deepened my appreciation for journalism’s role in times of crisis and amplifying writer’s voices. I discovered how meaningful it was to shape narratives, whether through my own writing or by giving feedback that sharpened another writer’s voice.

Parallel to this, I discovered The Pudding and the world of interactive, data-driven storytelling. The SBS Data Science and Journalism Academy gave me my first chance to combine coding with communication. For the first time, programming wasn’t just about making something work, but telling a story. Investigating hidden website structures to write crawling code, building interactive visualizations, and contextualizing data into narratives blended my computer science side and my love of writing.

Now, as we face the potential rise of authoritarianism accelerated by technology used for surveillance and censorship, I feel a growing urgency. I aspire to work in data and tech journalism: to humanize data points, uncover the hidden structures of technology, and tell stories that help society understand and resist these shifts. While no dataset can capture every voice, I hope my work will reflect the nuances of this moment in history and giving people the tools to question the systems around them.
